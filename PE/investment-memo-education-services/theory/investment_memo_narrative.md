# Investment Committee Memo — Horizon Learning Partners

**Target**: Horizon Learning Partners (hypothetical K-12 supplemental education / tutoring
platform) | **Sector**: Education Services | **Proposed Structure**: Leveraged buyout, 5-year hold

*This memo's narrative sections are illustrative and general — written to demonstrate the
structure and content of a real IC memo, not to make claims about any real company. The
quantitative exhibits below are generated by `code/investment_memo_model.py`; see
`output/` for the underlying tables and charts.*

## 1. Executive Summary

Horizon Learning Partners is proposed as a leveraged buyout at 8.5x EBITDA ($272mm Enterprise
Value), financed with 5.5x total leverage and a $101.4mm sponsor equity check. The base case
underwrites to a **3.34x MOIC / 27.3% IRR** over a 5-year hold; a stress-tested downside case
(reflecting a federal supplemental-funding cliff — see Section 3) still returns capital at
**1.67x MOIC / 10.9% IRR**, and an upside case reaches **4.49x MOIC / 35.0% IRR**. Full
scenario detail is in `output/scenario_summary.csv` and `output/scenario_comparison.png`.

## 2. Industry Overview (general, illustrative commentary)

The K-12 supplemental education / tutoring sector is shaped by a few well-known structural
dynamics, included here to illustrate the kind of context a real memo's industry section
would develop in far greater depth: (1) demand accelerated materially following pandemic-era
learning disruption, creating sustained district and family interest in remediation and
enrichment services; (2) a large share of near-term sector revenue for providers serving
public school districts has been tied to one-time federal COVID relief funding (ESSER —
Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief), which is a well-documented, time-limited
funding source rather than a permanent budget line — a real structural risk for the sector
broadly, not specific to any company; (3) the market remains fragmented, with regional and
subject-specific providers alongside a handful of larger national platforms, supporting
consolidation ("buy-and-build") theses similar to
[`PE/buy-and-build-healthcare-staffing`](../buy-and-build-healthcare-staffing); (4) hybrid
in-person/digital delivery models have become the norm since 2020, changing the labor and
technology cost structure of the sector; (5) demand is seasonally concentrated around the
school year and standardized testing calendars.

## 3. Key Risk Factors

- **Funding concentration / ESSER cliff risk**: to the extent a provider's district-contracted
  revenue relies on federal relief funding, a lapse in that funding is a well-documented,
  sector-wide near-term risk as of the mid-2020s. This project's **Downside scenario** models
  this explicitly — a demand shock in Year 2 (-3% revenue growth, margin compression) — and
  is the primary driver of why the downside case returns fall so far short of the base case
  (10.9% vs. 27.3% IRR) despite the deal still being capital-accretive.
- **Labor cost inflation**: tutor/instructor compensation is the largest cost line in this
  business model; competitive labor markets can pressure margins faster than pricing can adjust.
- **Regulatory/compliance exposure**: providers serving public school districts operate under
  state procurement and background-check/compliance requirements that add administrative cost
  and can slow growth via lengthy district contracting cycles.
- **Seasonality and working capital**: revenue and cash collections cluster around the school
  year, creating intra-year working capital swings that the model's revolver facility is
  specifically sized to absorb (see [`PE/full-lbo-model-business-services`](../full-lbo-model-business-services)
  for the revolver mechanics this model reuses).
- **Competitive risk from ed-tech**: lower-cost, technology-only tutoring alternatives could
  compress pricing power for providers with a more labor-intensive delivery model.

## 4. Management Assessment (illustrative)

A real memo's management section would assess: track record scaling prior locations/programs,
depth of the finance and operations bench beyond the founder, retention of instructional
talent, and quality of existing district/customer relationships and contract terms. Typical
red flags an IC would probe in a real process: over-reliance on a single founder for sales
relationships, thin financial reporting infrastructure relative to the scale of the proposed
deal, and any recent management or key-instructor turnover. None of this is modeled
quantitatively here — it's judgment-based diligence that sits alongside, not inside, the LBO
model, and would typically be documented in detail (reference calls, org chart, comp
benchmarking) before an IC vote.

## 6. Recommendation

Proceed to detailed diligence, with particular focus on (1) quantifying the specific share of
current revenue tied to time-limited federal funding sources, since the downside scenario's
severity is directly proportional to that exposure, and (2) management/finance-function
depth, given the operational complexity of running a multi-tranche leveraged capital structure
through a sector with meaningful revenue seasonality. The base case return profile (3.34x /
27.3% IRR) clears a typical buyout fund's return threshold with meaningful margin even under
the modeled downside, supporting continued pursuit subject to diligence findings.
